# Trust to Action

## Communications Operating Guide

**Purpose:** Help trusted organizations turn their mission, service, and earned reputation into useful communication that helps people find them, act, return, and stay connected.

> Good work should not remain hidden from the people who need it.

---

## 1. The Promise

Good businesses, churches, nonprofits, and community organizations need trust and connection to develop and keep relationships.

Our work translates mission and vision into usefulness. The message, offer, process, product, and follow-up should all help people:

- See the value
- Know whether it is for them
- Understand what happens next
- Take action with confidence
- Return, participate, or recommend it

We give clients the clarity, guidance, and care we want them to give the people they serve.

---

## 2. The Trust-to-Action Method

Use this sequence for outreach, websites, product pages, service descriptions, demonstrations, campaigns, and follow-up.

### 1. Name why the work matters

Start with the recipient, not the agency.

- Your service helps people find someone they can rely on.
- Your church carries the Gospel to families looking for a church home.
- Your program does more than provide food. It gives families hope in the midst of hunger.

### 2. Name the lived or observed pain

Use a real problem you have seen, experienced, or can verify.

- People have tried other providers and are still looking for someone they can trust.
- The organization is busy serving people and cannot spend every day marketing.
- Someone needs help but cannot find the hours, requirements, price, or next step.

Do not invent decline, lost revenue, dissatisfaction, or cultural crisis.

### 3. Show the trust gap

What do existing customers, members, or partners already know that a new person cannot see?

Common gaps:

- Strong reputation, weak website
- Good reviews, unclear services
- Active church, missing worship or prayer times
- Valuable program, confusing registration
- Strong referral, no page that confirms the recommendation

### 4. Make the offer useful

State what is being made or improved and what it will do.

- A service page that answers routine questions
- A church website with the Gospel, ministries, worship times, prayer times, and contact information
- A program page that explains who it serves and how to begin
- A booking, intake, or follow-up tool that reduces repeated work

### 5. Use emotional benefit laddering

Move through four levels:

**Service → Practical effect → Emotional consequence → Human value**

Examples:

- Auto repair → dependable transportation → relief → ability to work and care for family
- Food program → meals → relief from hunger → hope and stability
- Church information → confidence about visiting → connection → the Gospel and a church home
- Hair service → reliable care → confidence → readiness to move through the world

Elevate the meaning without exaggerating what one service can accomplish.

### 6. Name both benefits

Every offer should help two groups.

**For the organization:** less repeated work, clearer inquiries, completed bookings, retention, referrals, participation, or revenue.

**For the person:** less confusion, greater confidence, easier access, relief, belonging, or a clear next step.

### 7. End with an easy action

Use a low-pressure choice:

- I can send a sample, or we can talk through what would be most useful.
- Would you like to see an example first or discuss the project?
- Where would you like to start?

## 3. Voice

The core agency voice combines:

- **Bryan Stevenson:** moral clarity, dignity, calm authority
- **Fast Company:** accessible strategic explanation and practical innovation
- **Trader Joe's:** warm recommendation—“we found something useful worth sharing”

The result should feel human, direct, welcoming, and useful.

### Use

- Plain language
- Short sentences
- Specific observations
- Clear prices when the scope is defined
- Warm authority
- Respect for the work already being done
- Concrete next steps

### Avoid

- Discoverability
- Optimization
- Funnel
- Customer journey
- Digital transformation
- Modern website
- Ecosystem
- Unlock growth
- Passive leads
- Artificial urgency
- Generic praise
- Clinical or therapeutic language

Do not force this agency voice onto every product or client.

A plumber should sound practical. A mechanic should sound clear and reliable. A church should sound like the church. A game should be playful. A utility app should be functional. Voice serves the audience and the task.

---

## 4. Choose the Communication Intent

Before writing, choose one primary job:

- Introduce
- Explain
- Demonstrate
- Reassure
- Compare
- Invite
- Convert
- Welcome
- Retain
- Re-engage
- Refer
- Confirm

One asset should not try to do everything.

### Product description

Explain what it is, who it helps, what it does, and why it matters.

### Service description

Clarify who it is for, what is included, what changes, the process, price, and next step.

### Demo

Show the problem, action, result, and one practical benefit. Proof matters more than brand storytelling.

### Website

Confirm the visitor is in the right place, explain the offer, show evidence, and make one next action obvious.

### Social post

Give each post one job: educate, prove, demonstrate, invite, convert, or reconnect.

## 5. Channel Rules

### Google Business Profile and search listings

People are already looking. Prioritize accurate categories, services, hours, location, price cues, photos, reviews, and a direct booking or contact path.

### Website or landing page

Use clear hierarchy:

1. What this is
2. Who it helps
3. Why it matters
4. What is offered
5. Evidence
6. What happens next
7. One primary action

### Aggregators and marketplaces

People are comparing options. Lead with the exact service, location, availability, price, proof, and difference. Do not lead with abstract mission language.

### Email

Use a specific subject line, one relevant observation, one clear offer, and one easy question. Usually 80–180 words.

### Contact form

Keep it to 50–100 words. State why the message belongs there, name one observation, give one offer, and ask for the right person.

### Instagram or Facebook direct message

Keep it conversational and easy to answer. Usually 25–70 words. Do not paste a full email.

### Short video

Use:

1. Sharp opening
2. Familiar problem
3. Example or demonstration
4. Useful insight
5. One action

### App or game

Use the product's voice, not the founder's. Explain the function, experience, and next step. Let demonstrations and screenshots carry the proof.

## 6. Personalized Mass Communication

Use a blended model.

### Fixed

- Core offer
- Main benefit
- Price or starting point
- Call to action

### Variable

- Name
- Organization
- Service or program
- Location
- Publicly visible gap
- Existing proof

### Generated

- Opening line
- One observation
- One credible consequence
- One relevant sample or resource

Personalization must be public, relevant, recent, and non-invasive.

Say:

> I could not find your current prayer times online.

Do not say:

> Your church is losing members because your website is outdated.

---

## 7. Channel Priority

For each product, service, or vertical, choose three channels.

### #1 Capture existing intent

Where people already search:

- Google
- Maps
- App stores
- Aggregators
- Marketplaces
- Directories

### #2 Build trust and proof

Where people evaluate:

- Website
- Reviews
- Demonstrations
- Instagram
- Facebook
- YouTube
- Case studies

### #3 Build return and referral

Where relationships continue:

- Email
- Text
- In-app messages
- Membership
- Community groups
- Rebooking
- Follow-up and referral systems

Keep separate brand lanes when audiences, promises, prices, voices, or buying processes differ enough that mixing them creates confusion.

## 8. Live Market Tests

Do not let an LLM choose the winning message by preference. Test through real response.

### Core tests

1. Pain-led vs. outcome-led
2. Functional benefit vs. emotional benefit
3. Direct offer vs. sample-first
4. Authority statement vs. opening question
5. Mission-led vs. practical-led
6. Visible price vs. price after reply
7. Personalized observation vs. category observation
8. Short vs. medium length
9. Demo vs. explanation
10. Different calls to action

### Divergent tests

When small wording changes do not work, test different models:

- Educational tool vs. sales message
- Founder video vs. product demo
- Emotional story vs. practical checklist
- Direct price vs. diagnostic
- Search-first vs. social-first
- Website conversion vs. conversation conversion

### Measurement

Track:

- Sent or viewed
- Replies
- Qualified replies
- Clicks
- Bookings
- Purchases
- Registrations
- Revenue
- Return or retention
- Referrals

A message with fewer replies but stronger conversion and retention may be the better message.

Change one major variable at a time. Do not test deception, fake urgency, misleading prices, or invented proof.

## 9. Production Loop

1. Create one authoritative campaign brief.
2. Build one core asset.
3. Derive channel-specific versions.
4. Schedule controlled batches.
5. Track every version and source.
6. Pause weak assets.
7. Expand proven messages.
8. Add retention and referral communication.
9. Record what the market taught us.

### Campaign brief

- Brand or vertical
- Audience
- Intent
- Offer
- Pain
- Functional benefit
- Emotional benefit
- Evidence
- Price
- Primary action
- Retention goal
- Voice
- Primary, secondary, and retention channels
- Test variable

## 10. LLM Navigation Method

This document is an operating guide, not a prompt to repeat in full.

### First pass: extract

Identify only what matters for the current task:

- Brand or vertical
- Audience
- Intent
- Channel
- Offer
- Pain
- Functional benefit
- Emotional benefit
- Proof
- Action
- Retention goal
- Voice
- Test variable

### Second pass: execute

Create the smallest complete output that accomplishes the task. Adapt it to the channel. Remove jargon and unnecessary explanation.

### Third pass: verify when the work is consequential

Check claims, price, links, personalization, channel fit, next action, retention path, and measurement.

### Capacity rule

If context is limited, preserve these principles in order:

1. One strategy does not require one voice.
2. Match the audience, intent, and channel.
3. Name the functional and emotional benefits.
4. Make the offer and next step clear.
5. Use supported personalization only.
6. Test through real market behavior.
7. Measure conversion, retention, and referral.
8. Maintain one authoritative campaign source.

---

## Final Check

Before publishing or sending, confirm:

- Why does this work matter?
- Who is struggling, searching, or deciding?
- What have they likely experienced?
- What makes this organization worth trusting?
- What practical problem is solved?
- What deeper human value is supported?
- What becomes easier for the organization?
- What should the reader do next?
- Does the voice fit this specific brand and channel?
- Can the result be measured?

> Use mass production for scale, personalization for relevance, channel adaptation for fit, and real market response for learning.
